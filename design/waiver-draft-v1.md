# Design notes

## The tonal-break rule (load-bearing — do not "fix" this)

Everything above is ACME's cheerful corporate voice **except Section II and the Operator
Disclosure**, which are deliberately plain and deliberately out of character.

This is not a stylistic wobble. It is the answer to threat-model concern #2 in
`ethics-and-safety.md` — *the untrusted exit*: the game trains distrust of the real/fiction
boundary, so the OFF switch ends up living inside the one thing the player has been taught not to
believe. Wrapping the whole consent surface in a diegetic EULA makes that strictly worse. STOP has
to be unmistakably operative, and the way to make it unmistakable in a document where everything
else is in character is to **break character exactly once**.

It also happens to be the better horror. In a document where every line is a corporate performance,
the one passage that stops performing reads as the only true thing in it — which is precisely what
you want the safeword to read as.

Floor items 1 and 2 (`ethics-and-safety.md`) live in the break: a STOP that actually works, and
18+. Floor item 3 (distress detection) is what clause 9 and the last line of Section II are
promising on behalf of.

## Where "this is fiction" moved, and why

The old clause 2 carried both the fiction acknowledgment and the data disclosure. ACME would never
admit the Service is fiction — so the acknowledgment moved out of the clause list and into Section
II, where the real voice makes it. Clause 2 keeps the data half, which ACME would absolutely say.

This does not weaken the convincer. QMM's horror was never "the player believes ACME is a real
company"; it is the gap between knowing it's fiction and not believing it (old clause 2, now
Section II, unchanged in substance). Informed consent is what licenses the brutality — the doorknob
on the inside is what lets the house be dark.

## Clause 10 is still a placeholder, and the frame made it worse

It reads as a real release — release/waive/discharge, all claims, negligence, "to the fullest
extent permitted by law" — and it is **not legally vetted and not enforceable as written.**

The ACME frame actively degrades it: the released party is now a **fictional company**, and a
release running in favor of an entity that does not exist is worth less than the old version's
"the Story." Gross negligence and intentional harm generally cannot be waived anywhere, and a
release for deliberately inflicted psychological harm is novel ground to begin with.

The real operator entity (LLC per `ethics-and-safety.md`) has to be the named party before any
non-friend ring — which is what the Operator Disclosure placeholders exist to force. Register was
the goal here ("looks legal"), not enforceability. Clauses 1–9 remain assumption-of-risk /
consent / voluntariness, not a release.

## Planted details (deliberate — don't explain them, don't correct them)

- **Effective 14 March 2031** — a revision date that has not happened yet. Block universe. Noticeable
  if you look, invisible if you don't.
- **Rev. 4** — three revisions nobody saw.
- **The struck-through classification marking** and the retained hazard notice — ACME's consumer
  wrapper stretched over a government form, with the seams showing. ACME reproduced the notice "in
  full" because ACME does not understand it.
- **"the originating program"** in clause 10 — ACME indemnifying a party it cannot name.

An author may pull any of these threads. None of them should ever be resolved in-product; the
moment the fiction explains itself, the dread converts to trivia (`premise-and-branding.md`).

## Not done here — the channel lane owns it

This document is the spec. The **live** consent surface still runs the old participation-agreement
text (`qmm-web/public/index.html`, and whatever the Android app ships). Implementing it means new
copy, a letterhead that looks like ACME's own (**never** styled after Warner Bros.' — see the
branding doc's caveat), and visual separation for the tonal break so Section II reads as a
different voice on a phone screen. The ACME look does not exist yet; nobody has drawn it.
